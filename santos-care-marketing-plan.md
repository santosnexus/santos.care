# Marketing Plan: santos.care (Heal India Medi Tourism)

**Prepared:** June 2026
**Brand:** Heal India Medi Tourism — a division of Santos King Tours & Travels Pvt. Ltd. (santos.travel)
**Location:** Aluva, Kochi, Kerala
**Domain:** santos.care

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Heal India Medi Tourism positions itself as an end-to-end medical concierge for international patients seeking affordable, accredited treatment in India — spanning cardiology, orthopedics, IVF/fertility, oncology, cosmetic surgery, dental care, and neurology. The brand currently claims 10,000+ patients served, 500+ doctors, and 150+ partner hospitals, with testimonials drawn from the UK, UAE, and Kenya.

The opportunity is large and growing: India's medical tourism market is on a double-digit CAGR trajectory through the next decade, with international patients accounting for the large majority of sector revenue and demand diversifying into Africa, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia as some traditional source markets (notably Bangladesh) have softened. South India — Kerala's home region — is forecast to be one of the fastest-growing zones nationally. This plan focuses Santos's marketing effort on capturing that diversification wave, using Kochi's specific strengths (Kerala's wellness/Ayurveda heritage, Aster DM's home turf, Kochi International Airport connectivity to the Gulf and Africa) as a wedge against larger, metro-dominated facilitators.

---

## 2. Market Context

- India's medical tourism market is estimated around **USD 20 billion in 2026**, growing at roughly **12% CAGR**, with projections reaching the USD 60–65 billion range by the mid-2030s.
- **International patients drive the large majority of sector revenue**, with cardiovascular care the single largest treatment category by spend, and patients aged 36–55 the largest demographic cohort.
- Government-backed reforms are accelerating inbound flow: e-medical visas now cover **170+ countries** with approval cycles cut to 48–72 hours, directly lowering the friction Santos needs to market against.
- **Source-market diversification is the defining trend of 2026.** Political instability affecting Bangladesh and parts of West Asia has pushed major hospital groups (Fortis, Apollo, Aster) to actively court patients from Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Ethiopia, Uzbekistan, and Southeast Asia — the same geographies already represented in Santos's own testimonials.
- Competitive facilitator platforms (e.g., Vaidam) are using AI chatbots and virtual hospital tours to compress commissions to 8–12% and increase price transparency — a signal that **digital-first, transparent, conversational service** is becoming the baseline expectation, not a differentiator.
- Decentralization is a secondary trend: patients are increasingly open to tier-2 hubs offering lower accommodation costs and calmer recovery environments — a structural advantage for Kochi over Delhi/Mumbai/Chennai.

**Implication for Santos:** the market is shifting from pure cost-arbitrage messaging toward trust, transparency, and end-to-end journey management. A regional facilitator based in Kerala can win specific corridors (East Africa, GCC, CIS) rather than competing head-on with Apollo/Fortis/Medanta's national brand budgets.

## 4. Competitive Landscape

- **Hospital-direct brands** (Apollo, Fortis, Max, Medanta, Aster DM) — far larger budgets, but their marketing is hospital-first, not journey-first. Aster is Kerala's dominant name, which Santos should acknowledge rather than out-shout; partnering as a coordination layer across *multiple* hospitals (not tied to one chain) is the differentiator.
- **National facilitator platforms** (Vaidam and similar) — compete on AI-driven transparency and low commissions; strong SEO presence nationally.
- **Independent local agents/WhatsApp brokers** — common in Gulf and East African corridors, often informal and low-trust; Santos can win by being the *accredited, branded, reviewable* alternative to an anonymous broker.

**Positioning gap Santos can own:** "Kerala-based, multi-hospital medical concierge with a real local team and end-to-end visa-to-recovery support" — distinct from single-hospital marketing and from informal brokers.

## 5. Brand Positioning & Messaging

**Core positioning statement:**
*Heal India Medi Tourism is the patient's single point of contact for world-class, accredited treatment in India — handling everything from hospital selection to visa, stay, and recovery, so patients and families don't have to navigate it alone.*

**Messaging pillars:**
1. **Trust & accreditation** — JCI/NABH-accredited partner hospitals, named specialists, verifiable outcomes.
2. **Transparency** — published package pricing ranges, no hidden facilitator fees, free written treatment plans within 24 hours.
3. **Concierge, not just referral** — visa support, airport pickup, translators, accommodation, follow-up after the patient returns home.
4. **Local roots, global reach** — based in Kerala, serving 60+ countries; "we live where you're coming to."

**What to avoid:** leading with price alone (race-to-the-bottom against larger players) or overclaiming clinical outcomes (Santos is a facilitator, not a hospital — credibility depends on not overstepping into medical claims).

## 6. Channel Strategy

### 6.1 SEO & Content (highest long-term ROI, lowest cost)
- Treatment-specific landing pages already exist (cardiology, orthopedics, IVF, oncology, cosmetic, dental, neurology) — expand each with cost-comparison tables (India vs. US/UK/UAE), recovery timelines, and FAQ schema to win featured snippets.
- Build a content cluster around **"cost of [procedure] in India vs [country]"** — this query pattern is exactly how price-sensitive patients search.
- Publish patient-journey blog content by source country ("Medical visa guide for Kenyan patients," "IVF treatment guide for UAE residents") to capture long-tail, high-intent search and double as shareable WhatsApp content.
- Add structured outcome/testimonial pages with patient consent — video testimonials convert far better than text in this category.

### 6.2 Conversational / WhatsApp (the actual point of sale)
- WhatsApp is already a stated channel (`wa.me` link live on-site) — this is consistent with how Gulf and African patients actually transact. Treat it as the primary conversion surface, not an afterthought to the contact form.
- Standardize a first-response flow: treatment interest → upload reports → free treatment plan within 24 hours, with a human handoff for anything clinical. A lightweight AI-assisted triage bot (qualifying country, treatment type, urgency before routing to a human coordinator) can cut response time without sacrificing trust — but every clinical or pricing commitment should be human-reviewed before it's sent, given the sensitivity of medical advice.
- Localize WhatsApp greetings/templates by source-market language (Arabic, Swahili, French) to match the multilingual push the government's own medical visa portal is already making.

### 6.3 Paid Social & Search
- Meta (Facebook/Instagram) ads targeted by country to GCC, Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Nigeria, and UK diaspora — testimonial-style video ads outperform static creative in this category.
- Google Ads on high-intent, procedure + cost keywords; pair with the cost-comparison landing pages above so ad and landing page intent match.
- Retarget website visitors who viewed treatment pages but didn't message — WhatsApp click-to-chat retargeting converts well for this audience.

### 6.4 Partnerships & Referral
- Formal referral agreements with diaspora community associations, churches, and Malayali associations in the Gulf and East Africa — these communities already function as informal referral networks; a small referral incentive (or simply excellent service plus a referral ask) can formalize this at low cost.
- Cross-promote with santos.travel for patients who want to combine recovery with light tourism/wellness — Kerala's existing wellness-tourism reputation is a free asset.
- Explore listing on India's official "Heal in India" medical value travel portal, which is positioning itself as a real-time booking marketplace in 2026 — early presence there is low-cost and government-endorsed.

### 6.5 PR & Trust-Building
- Pursue coverage in diaspora media (Gulf-based Malayali newspapers, East African Indian-community outlets) rather than competing for mainstream Indian healthcare press dominated by big hospital chains.
- Publish an annual transparency report (patients served, average savings, outcomes by category) — this directly counters the "informal broker" trust gap.

## 7. Conversion Funnel

1. **Awareness** — SEO content, paid social, diaspora partnerships.
2. **Interest** — Treatment page → "Get Free Treatment Plan" / WhatsApp click-to-chat.
3. **Consideration** — Patient uploads reports → free treatment plan + cost estimate within 24 hours (already a stated promise — protect this SLA operationally, since it's the brand's core trust mechanism).
4. **Decision** — Hospital/doctor selection, visa assistance, package confirmation.
5. **Treatment & Stay** — Concierge touchpoints (airport, accommodation, translator) — each is a content/testimonial opportunity if documented with consent.
6. **Post-care & Advocacy** — Follow-up check-ins, review request, referral ask.

The biggest leverage point is step 3: speed and quality of the first treatment plan response is what separates a trustworthy facilitator from a broker. This should be the single most protected operational metric.

## 8. 90-Day Roadmap

**Days 1–30: Foundation**
- Audit and tighten the 24-hour treatment-plan response SLA; document the WhatsApp intake flow.
- Build 3–5 "cost in India vs. [country]" cost-comparison pages targeting priority corridors (UAE, Kenya, UK).
- Launch first round of testimonial video collection from recent patients (with consent).

**Days 31–60: Channel activation**
- Launch Meta ad campaigns in 2–3 priority countries with testimonial creative, driving to WhatsApp.
- Begin outreach to 3–5 diaspora community organizations for referral partnerships.
- Submit/verify listing on the government's medical value travel portal.

**Days 61–90: Optimize & scale**
- Review channel performance (cost per qualified lead, lead-to-consultation rate) and reallocate budget toward the best-performing country/channel combination.
- Expand content cluster based on top-performing search queries.
- Formalize a referral-incentive program if partnership pilots show traction.

## 11. Key Risks

- **Geopolitical volatility** in source markets (West Asia instability has already disrupted larger hospitals' patient flow in 2026) — mitigate by diversifying across 4–5 source countries rather than over-indexing on one.
- **Margin compression** as national platforms push commissions down via AI-driven transparency — Santos's concierge depth (not just referral) is the defense, but it must be visibly marketed, not just operationally true.
- **Trust deficit vs. informal brokers** — every transparency and accreditation signal compounds here; this is worth over-investing in relative to paid acquisition.
